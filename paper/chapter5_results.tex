\section{Main Findings}
\label{sec:findings}

\index{results!main findings}

The experimental evaluation of the QRC-EV framework yields four principal
findings:

\index{findings!summary}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Stateful QHMM significantly outperforms stateless QRC on
    memory-demanding tasks.} On NARMA-10, the stateful QHMM-20q achieves
    R²=0.86, compared to R²=0.75 for stateless QRC-20q and R²=0.52 for
    ESN. This demonstrates that the depolarizing-channel state maintenance
    is critical for temporal tasks requiring explicit memory of 10+
    timesteps.

    \index{NARMA-10!QHMM advantage}

  \item \textbf{Quantum advantage is task-dependent and qubit-scaled.} The
    Hilbert space dimension ($2^n$) translates to approximately $n$ bits
    of effective temporal memory. For tasks requiring short memory
    (Mackey-Glass, weekly pattern), 8-qubit reservoirs suffice; for
    NARMA-10 (memory-10), 20 qubits significantly outperform 8 qubits.
    Beyond 12 qubits, stateless QRC degrades while stateful QHMM continues
    improving.

    \index{qubit scaling!memory}
    \index{quantum advantage!qubit-dependent}

  \item \textbf{Linear models remain competitive on real-world data.}
    Ridge regression achieves R²=0.91 on real EV charging load data,
    significantly exceeding all reservoir and QHMM variants (best:
    QHMM-20q at R²=0.58). This suggests that for well-structured,
    noise-regularized real-world time series, the additional complexity
    of quantum reservoirs does not translate to practical advantage.

    \index{real-world!linear model sufficient}
    \index{EV charging!Ridge dominance}

  \item \textbf{QHMM is unstable on simple periodic signals.} Both
    QHMM-8q and QHMM-20q collapse on the sinusoidal dataset (R² $< -1$),
    while all other methods achieve R² $\geq 0.96$. This failure mode
    indicates that the OOM discretization is poorly matched to signals
    with simple spectral structure, where the state space quantization
    introduces artifacts.

    \index{QHMM!failure mode!periodic signals}
\end{enumerate}

\index{sinusoidal!QHMM failure}

\section{Comparison with Prior Work}

\index{related work!comparison}

Our results extend prior QRC benchmarks in several ways. The
survey of \citet{gosavic2024quantum} identified a consistent gap between
controlled QRC experiments and real-world forecasting tasks. Our evaluation
confirms this gap: while QRC and QHMM achieve strong results on synthetic
tasks (Mackey-Glass R²=0.99, NARMA-10 R²=0.86), the advantage over
classical methods on real EV data is marginal or absent. This underscores
the importance of evaluating quantum reservoir methods on authentic
data pipelines rather than synthetic benchmarks alone.

\index{benchmark gap}
\index{synthetic vs real-world}

The qubit scaling results (Figure~\ref{fig:narma10_scaling}) confirm and
quantify the observation of \citet{fujii2017keep} that the stateless QRC
memory capacity saturates and declines beyond a qubit threshold, while
stateful reservoirs continue to benefit from additional qubits. The
cross-over point at $nQ \approx 10$ for NARMA-10 is consistent with the
theoretical prediction that the effective memory capacity of a stateless
QRC is $\mathcal{O}(nQ)$ bits while the circuit depth grows with $nQ$.

\index{fujii2017keep}
\index{stateless memory saturation}

\index{circuit depth!scaling}

\section{Implications for EV Charging Forecasting}

\index{EV charging!implications}
\index{practical implications}

For the specific task of EV charging load forecasting, our results
suggest the following guidance:

\index{decision guide}
\index{method selection}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{For short-term load balancing} (1-5 minute ahead):
    Ridge regression on lagged features is sufficient and computationally
    efficient. The EV charging process is dominated by daily periodicities
    that are well-captured by linear regression.

    \index{short-term forecasting}

  \item \textbf{For day-ahead planning} (24h horizon): The QHMM-20q
    provides a marginal improvement over classical methods (R²=0.58 vs
    R²=0.53 for ESN), at the cost of significantly higher computational
    overhead. Whether this improvement justifies the cost depends on the
    economic value of forecast accuracy for the specific application.

    \index{day-ahead planning}
    \index{cost-benefit}

  \item \textbf{For anomaly detection and demand response}: Tasks requiring
    detection of unusual charging patterns (e.g., fleet charging spikes)
    may benefit from the QHMM's stateful state tracking. This remains
    an open question for future work.

    \index{anomaly detection!future work}
\end{itemize}

\index{future work!EV applications}

\section{Limitations}

\index{limitations}

Several important limitations of this work should be noted:

\index{limitations!QG reservoir}
\index{limitations!GPU simulation}
\index{limitations!discretization}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{GPU simulation, not physical quantum hardware.} All
    experiments use CUDA Quantum simulation on NVIDIA GPUs. While this
    provides reproducible results, it does not demonstrate advantage on
    actual quantum hardware, where gate errors and decoherence would
    degrade performance. Physical hardware experiments via Amazon Braket
    are planned as future work.

    \index{physical quantum hardware!future}

  \item \textbf{State discretization via binning.} The mapping from
    continuous reservoir states to discrete QHMM outcomes is a simple
    quantile binning, which may discard useful state information. More
    sophisticated discretization methods (e.g., learned codebooks) could
    improve QHMM performance.

    \index{disambiguation!future work}
    \index{codebook learning!future work}

  \item \textbf{Single EV network.} The real-world evaluation is limited
    to the Palo Alto ACN dataset. Performance may differ on other
    networks with different charging patterns (e.g., workplace charging,
    fast-charging stations).

    \index{generalization!network-specific}

  \item \textbf{No hyperparameter tuning for QHMM.} The QHMM hyperparameters
    (state dimension $S=2$, trace decays $\lambda_f=\lambda_b=0.8$) were
    not systematically tuned. Larger state dimensions and adaptive trace
    parameters may improve results.

    \index{hyperparameter tuning!future}
    \index{state dimension!S=2}
\end{enumerate}

\index{future work!hyperparameters}

\index{method selection!summary table}

The QRC-EV framework demonstrates that quantum reservoir computing,
when combined with stateful QHMM modeling, can provide competitive
performance on temporal learning tasks requiring explicit memory.
However, the conditions under which quantum methods outperform
well-tuned classical alternatives remain narrow and task-specific.
The framework provides a rigorous evaluation platform for continued
investigation of quantum temporal learning.
